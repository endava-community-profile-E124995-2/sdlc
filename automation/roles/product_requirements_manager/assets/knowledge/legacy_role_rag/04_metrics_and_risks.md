# Product & Requirements Manager — Metrics and Risks

This consolidated execution reference defines the metrics, outcomes, risks, constraints, dependencies, and control artifacts relevant to the Product & Requirements Manager.

It covers:

- `metric`: A measure used to evaluate outcomes, delivery health, quality, or change.
- `risk`: A failure mode, exposure, or uncertainty the role monitors or mitigates.
- Supporting execution context: constraints, dependencies, and control artifacts.

Use this file when the question is about measurement, success criteria, delivery exposure, governance exposure, or execution controls.

## Evidence and Traceability Rules

- Every meaningful requirement should trace back to a problem statement, source evidence, business rule or constraint, decision record, and intended metric or outcome.
- Separate `fact`, `inference`, and `assumption` explicitly. Facts come from observed evidence, inferences interpret that evidence, and assumptions fill the remaining gap.
- Record confidence when evidence is incomplete and note what would materially increase or decrease confidence.
- Make unknowns visible. An unresolved question is part of the work product, not something to hide.
- Keep change rationale explicit. When scope, priority, or requirement wording changes, capture what changed, why, and what downstream impact is expected.
- Link launch and post-launch review back to the original expected result so the team can compare promise against outcome.
- Prefer fewer high-signal links over broad weak traceability. Traceability should explain the work, not become paperwork theater.

## Generic Anti-Patterns and Failure Modes

- Jumping to solution definition before problem validation.
- Treating stakeholder requests as product requirements without evidence.
- Writing requirements that are ambiguous, untestable, or disconnected from business rules.
- Mixing product intent with implementation detail so early that scope becomes brittle.
- Using vanity metrics instead of outcome-bearing measures.
- Ignoring dependencies, constraints, or approval paths until delivery is already blocked.
- Allowing changes without rationale, impact assessment, or decision traceability.
- Optimizing for launch output while neglecting adoption, support burden, or outcome learning.
- Confusing loud signals with representative signals in customer or stakeholder feedback.
- Over-documenting low-value work while under-documenting high-risk or cross-functional work.
