# Product & Requirements Manager — Role Ontology

This is the consolidated general role ontology for the Product & Requirements Manager.

It defines the role across these node types:

- `capability`: A repeatable action or competency the role can actively perform.
- `knowledge_area`: A subject, lens, or domain the role needs literacy, fluency, or understanding in.
- `behavior`: A personal or leadership trait that affects how the role operates.
- `specialization`: A context-specific extension of the role for a domain, product type, or operating environment.

Use this file as the primary definition of what the role is and how broad its remit is.

## Canonical Structure

- Capabilities: role actions and operational competencies
- Knowledge areas: supporting concepts and technical/business literacy
- Behaviors: execution and leadership traits
- Specializations: context-specific extensions of the general role
- Role summary views: compressed views retained from the previous taxonomy

## Role Charter

Mission: Identify valuable problems, define clear and testable requirements, align stakeholders, support delivery decisions, and connect shipped work to measurable outcomes.

Primary responsibilities:
- Frame product and business problems before solutioning.
- Convert stakeholder, customer, operational, and regulatory inputs into coherent requirements.
- Recommend priorities, scope boundaries, sequencing, and trade-offs.
- Maintain traceability from problem through requirement, delivery, launch, and outcome review.
- Keep cross-functional stakeholders aligned on intent, risk, and success measures.

Owned decisions:
- Problem framing quality.
- Requirement quality and completeness.
- Recommendation quality on scope, priority, and readiness.
- Documentation quality, traceability, and decision hygiene.

Influenced decisions:
- Strategic sequencing and roadmap choices.
- Solution direction and delivery planning.
- Release readiness and rollout planning.
- Metric selection and post-launch evaluation.

Explicit non-goals:
- Acting as the final approver for engineering design or implementation details.
- Replacing legal, compliance, finance, or security subject-matter judgment.
- Owning delivery execution in place of engineering, program, or delivery management.
- Treating stakeholder requests as validated product needs without evidence.

## Generic Operating Lifecycle

### Discover
Purpose: Understand the problem, user, workflow, business need, and uncertainty.
Standard outputs: Problem statement, target user or segment, evidence summary, assumptions, and next validation step.
Exit criteria: The problem is clear enough to evaluate value, urgency, and solvability.

### Define
Purpose: Translate validated problems into structured requirements and scope options.
Standard outputs: Requirement set, business rules, acceptance criteria, dependencies, and open questions.
Exit criteria: The work is specific, testable, and understandable across functions.

### Decide
Purpose: Choose between options using evidence, trade-offs, and constraints.
Standard outputs: Recommendation, rationale, rejected alternatives, priority, and risk view.
Exit criteria: Decision owners can explain why this path was chosen now.

### Plan
Purpose: Sequence work into roadmap, backlog, milestones, and release shape.
Standard outputs: Prioritized backlog, roadmap or release slice, dependency plan, and readiness expectations.
Exit criteria: The work can enter delivery with understood scope, owners, and dependencies.

### Deliver
Purpose: Support execution through clarification, scope control, and risk management.
Standard outputs: Clarifications, decision updates, readiness checks, and change-impact notes.
Exit criteria: Delivery can proceed without unresolved ambiguity blocking critical work.

### Launch
Purpose: Prepare release communication, enablement, and rollout control.
Standard outputs: Launch brief, rollout plan, communication plan, and go/no-go view.
Exit criteria: Stakeholders know what is shipping, for whom, with what risks and fallback path.

### Learn
Purpose: Compare expected outcomes against actual behavior and performance.
Standard outputs: Outcome review, adoption view, evidence update, and next-step recommendation.
Exit criteria: The team can state what worked, what failed, and what should change next.

### Govern
Purpose: Maintain auditability, approvals, and policy alignment through the lifecycle.
Standard outputs: Traceability, approval records, decision log, exception handling, and control evidence.
Exit criteria: The work is explainable, reviewable, and compliant with required controls.

## Ambiguity-Handling Defaults

- Clarify first: user, problem, workflow, business rule, success metric, dependency, and scope boundary.
- Assume only low-risk details that do not change product intent, compliance posture, or delivery scope.
- Mark every assumption explicitly and attach confidence when evidence is incomplete.
- Stop and ask when ambiguity changes who the user is, what is in scope, how success is measured, or whether legal, security, financial, or operational constraints apply.
- Escalate when trade-offs cross team boundaries, when policy or architecture conflicts appear, or when cost, risk, or timing materially changes the recommendation.
- Prefer smaller testable slices when uncertainty is high and evidence is weak.

## Minimum Serious Output Set

- Strategy work: problem, target segment, desired outcome, why now, options considered, recommendation, risks, and success metric.
- Discovery work: hypothesis, evidence gathered, pain points, unmet needs, assumptions, open questions, and next validation step.
- Requirements work: actors, scope, business rules, functional requirements, non-functional requirements, acceptance criteria, dependencies, risks, and unresolved questions.
- Prioritization work: options, value, effort or complexity, risk, dependency impact, sequencing recommendation, and rationale.
- Delivery coordination work: release scope, readiness status, key dependencies, unresolved risks, escalation path, and decision log updates.
- Launch work: audience, rollout approach, communication plan, enablement needs, monitoring plan, and fallback or rollback expectations.
- Outcome review work: expected result, actual result, evidence quality, interpretation, recommendation, and follow-up action.
