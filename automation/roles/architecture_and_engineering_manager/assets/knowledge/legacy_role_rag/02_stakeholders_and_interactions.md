# Product & Requirements Manager — Stakeholders and Interactions

This is the consolidated stakeholder and interaction model for the Product & Requirements Manager.

It defines:

- `stakeholder`: A person, team, group, or forum the role interacts with.
- `interaction`: A reusable reason or mode of engagement with a stakeholder.

Use this file to understand who the role works with, why those interactions happen, and how the stakeholder landscape changes across phases of work.

## Stakeholder Interface Contracts

### Executive and Business Leadership
Typical input: Strategic direction, constraints, escalation context, investment expectations, and success criteria.
Typical output expected from the role: Clear recommendation, priority view, trade-offs, risks, and outcome framing.
Escalation trigger: Priority conflict, funding conflict, major risk, or decision latency that blocks progress.

### Product Peers and Product Leadership
Typical input: Product intent, backlog context, portfolio dependencies, and roadmap constraints.
Typical output expected from the role: Refined scope, requirement quality, decision framing, and alignment-ready documentation.
Escalation trigger: Conflicting product narratives, unclear ownership, or unresolved scope overlap across initiatives.

### Engineering and Architecture
Typical input: Feasibility feedback, technical constraints, dependency impact, quality and operability concerns.
Typical output expected from the role: Clear requirements, business logic, acceptance criteria, scope boundaries, and priority rationale.
Escalation trigger: Architecture conflict, non-functional gap, unresolved ambiguity, or dependency risk that changes delivery shape.

### Design and Research
Typical input: User insight, experience hypotheses, workflow observations, and usability concerns.
Typical output expected from the role: Problem framing, requirement intent, constraints, prioritization rationale, and validation focus.
Escalation trigger: Requirement intent diverges from user evidence or the experience risk is not reflected in scope.

### Data and Analytics
Typical input: Metric feasibility, instrumentation constraints, baseline performance, and observed behavior patterns.
Typical output expected from the role: Metric definitions, event requirements, success thresholds, and outcome questions worth answering.
Escalation trigger: No measurable success path, weak instrumentation, or disagreement on metric interpretation.

### Delivery, Program, and Coordination Roles
Typical input: Capacity, release timing, dependency status, process constraints, and readiness signals.
Typical output expected from the role: Prioritized scope, change-impact clarification, risk articulation, and decision hygiene.
Escalation trigger: Scope volatility, unresolved blockers, or timing pressure that invalidates the current plan.

### Go-To-Market, Sales, Customer Success, and Support
Typical input: Customer signals, adoption barriers, rollout needs, supportability concerns, and market pressure.
Typical output expected from the role: Prioritization rationale, requirement implications, launch intent, and expectation-setting language.
Escalation trigger: Sales pressure distorts roadmap logic, support burden is ignored, or rollout readiness is materially weak.

### Legal, Compliance, Security, and Finance
Typical input: Policy requirements, control expectations, contractual constraints, risk posture, and financial limits.
Typical output expected from the role: Traceable requirements, documented assumptions, impact assessment, and approval-ready summaries.
Escalation trigger: Policy conflict, unresolved compliance interpretation, or budget and contract constraints that alter scope or timing.

### External Customers, Partners, and Ecosystem Actors
Typical input: Workflow realities, integration constraints, adoption feedback, commercial pressure, and ecosystem needs.
Typical output expected from the role: Clear requirement interpretation, boundary-setting, rollout expectations, and feedback synthesis.
Escalation trigger: Customer-specific demands distort the general product direction or partner constraints materially affect delivery.
